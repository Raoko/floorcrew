    The Patterns That Separate Fast from Slow

    Fast engineers do the ugly version first.

    Hardcoded data before the data loader. print() before formatted output. Get it working in any form, then make it right.

    Fast engineers don't build infrastructure they haven't needed yet.

    No venv setup before there's Python code. No requirements.txt before there's an import. No run.sh before there's an app to run. Every file created before it's necessary is time spent on something that isn't the core.

    Fast engineers commit working checkpoints.

    Every time something works — even slightly — commit. It means you can always go back. It means you can experiment without fear.

    Fast engineers cut aggressively and unapologetically.

    Zone filter dropdown? Cut. Verbose mode? Cut. Favicon? Cut. Keyboard shortcuts? Cut. If it doesn't appear in the 3-minute demo, it doesn't exist. The app is: type a cable ID, see risk level, see MAINT snippet. That's it.

    Fast engineers don't context-switch mid-task.

    When you're writing impact.py, you are writing impact.py. Not thinking about Flask routes. Not thinking about slide design. The task list has 106 items. On a given day, care about exactly the next 3.

    Hackathon Meta
    How it actually works. What serious competitors do. How to think before, during, and after.
    Most people treat a hackathon like a sprint. The people who win treat it like a chess match where every move is made under time pressure. The meta is learnable. Here it is.

    Phase 1 — Pre-Hackathon (weeks before)

    What Strong Competitors Do Before Day 1

    The misconception is that the hackathon starts when the clock does. It doesn't. The best teams are already in motion 2–3 weeks out. Not building the actual submission — building their ability to build fast.

    They study the judging criteria first.

    Not the problem space. The judging criteria. Before they touch a keyboard, they can answer: What does winning look like in the eyes of the judges? What's the scoring rubric? What do past winners have in common? The idea that serves the rubric beats the technically superior idea that doesn't.

    They lock the domain, not the solution.

    Two weeks out, strong teams decide: "We're doing something in [X] domain" — not "we're building [specific thing]." This lets them read, explore, and surface real pain without locking into a bad idea too early. The specific solution crystallizes at the last possible responsible moment.

    They build their data assets.

    Static JSON, CSVs, mock databases — anything realistic that makes the demo feel real. This work is not the app, but it's what makes the app demoable in the 48-hour window. The teams that hit a working demo in hour 4 already had their data on day 1.

    They pre-decide their stack.

    No debate during the hackathon about what language, what framework, what database. Decided. Written down. Everyone on the team knows. Debate is expensive. Resolution is cheap when done ahead of time.

    They scout teammates early.

    Solo hackathon entries exist, but the winning formula is almost always 2–3 people with complementary skills and zero ego about who does what. The scout happens in advance — not 10 minutes before the kickoff.

    They draft the demo before writing code.

    The best teams write the demo script — the 3-minute story they'll tell — before the hackathon starts. Not the slides. The script. "We open here, we show this, the judge sees this, we close with this." Then the code is written to make that script true.

    Pre-hackathon prep timeline:
    3 weeks out Read judging criteria. Study past winners. Decide domain.
    2 weeks out Lock team. Scout real pain points. Generate 5–10 idea candidates.
    1 week out Validate top ideas. Kill weak ones. Lock the one. Draft demo script.
    3 days out Prep data assets. Pre-decide stack. Write skeleton code if allowed.
    Day 1 Clock starts. You already know what you're building.

    Phase 2 — How a Hackathon Actually Works

    The Real Structure

    Most hackathons follow the same rhythm regardless of theme or company. Understanding the rhythm lets you pace correctly instead of burning out at the wrong moment.

    Kickoff (Hour 0–1)

    Announcements, theme reveal, rules clarification. Strong teams use this time to confirm their plan is still valid — not to discover their plan. If the theme or rules change something, they adapt immediately, in minutes, not hours. Everyone else is still processing.

    The Dangerous Middle (Hour 2–12)

    This is where most teams die. They're building, but they haven't validated that what they're building will make a good demo. They go deep on the wrong feature. They argue about architecture. They scope-creep upward.

    Strong teams run a different process: they build the thinnest possible version of the core, show it to someone, watch the reaction, and cut or pivot. They do this loop every 3–4 hours. By hour 12, they've already killed 2 features that felt important at hour 2.

    The Turn (Hour 12–24)

    This is when you separate from the field. By now, weaker teams are tired, behind, and realizing their idea is more complex than expected. Strong teams are refining because they built the core early.

    This is also when the narrative gets locked. What is the demo flow? What's the one sentence? What's the before/after story? The team that can answer these clearly by hour 18 wins the room.

    Final Sprint (Hour 24–48)

    Polish, not features. Bug fixes on the demo path only. The rule: if the bug doesn't appear in the demo, it doesn't get fixed. Every minute is either rehearsal or story refinement. Strong teams spend 30% of this window doing nothing but running through the demo out loud.

    Judging

    Judges see 10–30 demos in a row. They're tired. They're pattern-matching. The demo that stands out is the one that makes the problem visceral in the first 30 seconds, shows a working thing (not a mockup), and has a clean "so what" at the end. That's the whole game.

    Phase 3 — Competitive Tricks

    The Moves That Actually Separate Winners

    Trick 1: Write the README before you write the code.

    The README forces you to explain what you built, why it matters, and how to run it — in plain language. If you can't write a clear README for an idea, you don't understand the idea well enough to build it. Write it first, and let the gaps tell you what to figure out.

    Trick 2: Make the demo path bulletproof, then stop.

    There is a set of exact steps a judge will take when you present. That path needs to work flawlessly. Everything else — edge cases, alt flows, error handling — is irrelevant. The code outside the demo path doesn't need to exist.

    Trick 3: Use real-sounding fake data.

    Judges unconsciously evaluate credibility. "FBS01_UP_A → DSR1" reads as real. "TestDevice1 → TestDevice2" reads as toy. Your static data should use real naming conventions, real-ish numbers, and real-looking structures. Twenty minutes spent making data look authentic is worth more than two hours of extra features.

    Trick 4: Name your pain point before you name your solution.

    The pitch structure that loses: "We built [tool] that does [thing]." The pitch structure that wins: "Right now, every time a DCT pulls a cable, they're guessing. There's no fast way to answer: what's the blast radius? We built the answer." Problem first, solution second. Always.

    Trick 5: Have a villager story.

    The most persuasive moment in any hackathon pitch is when someone says "this happened to me on the floor last Tuesday." A single specific real story is worth 10 slides of analysis. If you can ground the problem in something that actually happened — with a date, a rack number, a person — you win the credibility room before the demo even starts.

    Trick 6: Rehearse the failure case.

    What do you say if the demo breaks? Strong teams rehearse the failure out loud: "If the app goes down, I'll switch to the CLI. If the CLI breaks, I'll show the output file. If that breaks, I'll walk through the code." Having a graceful downgrade plan communicates confidence and preparation even when things go wrong.

    Trick 7: Control the clock in your demo.

    Every second of a 3-minute demo is yours. Don't ad-lib. Don't answer questions mid-demo. Don't let a judge's reaction throw you off the script. Run the demo, hit the beats, then open for questions. The teams that lose control of their demo time lose the room.

    Trick 8: End on the vision, not the current state.

    "Today it uses static JSON. Next step is live NetBox integration. After that, Slack bot alerts when risk changes." One sentence each. You're telling judges: this is real, this is a foundation, this scales. The current state is the proof of concept. The vision is why they should care.

    Phase 4 — Thinking Strategy

    The Mental Models That Win

    Demo-driven development.

    Write the demo first. Then write the code to make the demo true. Every feature gets evaluated by one question: does it make the demo better? If not, it doesn't get built. This sounds obvious. Almost nobody does it.

    Scope gravity is real — fight it actively.

    Ideas naturally expand. Features get added. "It would be great if..." is the most dangerous phrase in a hackathon. Every addition needs to pass: will this appear in the demo? Does this make the core story clearer? If the answer to both is no, cut it, even if it's good. Especially if it's good. Good-but-off-topic features steal time from great-and-on-topic features.

    Confidence is a strategy, not a feeling.

    The team that presents with certainty — even about an imperfect product — beats the team that hedges. "We know this uses static data, and that's intentional — it means it works anywhere, with no dependencies, and can be demoed on any machine." Own your constraints. Don't apologize for them.

    The audience is the judge, not the other teams.

    In the room, it's tempting to benchmark against other demos. Don't. You can't change what they built. You can only control your story and your clarity. Build for the judges, not against the competitors.

    Energy management is a technical skill.

    At hour 20, your judgment degrades. You'll add a feature that wastes 3 hours and doesn't matter. Strong teams know this and create a rule: after hour 16, no new features. Only polish on existing work. This sounds conservative. It's actually aggressive — it protects your most important hours for the work that matters most.

    The 80/20 of hackathon prep.

    80% of your outcome is determined by: (1) how well you understand the judging criteria, (2) how real your pain point is, (3) how clean your demo path is. The remaining 20% is code quality, design polish, and everything else. Most teams invert this and spend 80% on implementation and 20% on story. That's why most teams don't win.

    The one-sentence meta: The best hackathon submissions are not the most technically impressive — they're the ones where a tired judge watches a 3-minute demo and immediately understands why it matters, believes it works, and can explain it to someone else in a single sentence.

    Corporate Hackathon — What's Different

    External hackathons (HackMIT, TechCrunch Disrupt) optimize for novelty and market potential. Internal corporate hackathons optimize for different things — and the playbook shifts.

    Domain knowledge is your moat.

    In a public hackathon, anyone can understand the problem. In an internal hackathon, the people who live the problem have a massive advantage. "Only someone on the floor would think of this" is not just a pitch line — it's a genuine defensibility argument. Lean into it hard.

    Judges know the company's pain.

    Corporate judges have seen the dashboards, heard the complaints, attended the postmortems. You don't have to explain why the problem matters — they already know. Your job is to show that you built something real that addresses it. The credibility bar for "this is a real problem" is low. The bar for "this actually works" is high.

    Politics are real but manageable.

    Projects that require crossing team boundaries, accessing other teams' data, or changing existing workflows will face silent resistance from judges who own those systems. The safest path: build something that is entirely self-contained, needs no buy-in from other teams to demo, and could be handed to anyone to run on their laptop in 5 minutes. Independence wins in corporate settings.

    The OKR alignment question will come up.

    Every judge in a corporate hackathon is thinking: "How does this connect to what the company cares about?" Have a one-liner ready. Not a fake one — a real connection. "This directly reduces MAINT ticket creation time, which maps to DCT efficiency and uptime reliability." That's the answer. Know it cold.

    What Judges Want to Hear

    • Who feels the pain? (specific person, specific moment)
    • What does it look like before your tool? (concrete, not abstract)

    • How would this scale? (one sentence, believable)
    • What's next? (one concrete next step, not a laundry list)
